# 📝 Full Context

Evan Spiegel, CEO and co-founder of Snap Inc., is one of the very few humans in the world who has successfully built and scaled a lasting consumer social app. In the 15 years since Snapchat launched, essentially zero social consumer apps have launched and stuck around—yet Snapchat has over 1 billion monthly active users, generates over $6 billion a year in revenue, and people post over 8 billion AR lens photos a day. This interview covers why consumer social products are so hard to build, Snap's innovation track record (Stories, AR glasses, swipe navigation), their 12-year hardware journey with Spectacles, how AI is changing their design and product development process, and why this year is a "crucible moment" for the company. The overall theme: humanity matters more than technology in determining what sticks, and distribution—not product features—is the new moat.

---

# 🏛️ Their AI Thesis

> 🏛️ **Humanity dictates how technology is adopted, not the other way around.** While the industry focuses obsessively on technology advances, Spiegel believes we're entering a period of massive societal pushback against AI changes. Technology leaders assume people will blindly adopt new technology, but that's not how humans work. At Snap, this manifests in using AI to enable designers to ship code and build products faster, while using jobs-to-be-done frameworks to stay focused on what matters to actual humans—not just what the technology can do.

# 💭 Key Mental Models

---

### 🔄 Model 1: Distribution as the New Moat

> 🧠 **The Concept:** In the early days of mobile, people downloaded new apps constantly. Today, they don't. The hardest part of building consumer products isn't product-market fit—it's distribution. TikTok spent billions subsidizing both creators and viewers to bootstrap their ecosystem. Threads leveraged Meta's existing cross-product distribution. Snap figured out a different path: connecting people not to ALL their friends, but to their CLOSE friends—the people they care about most.

> 💡 **The Truth:** Network effects alone aren't enough. Software features are trivially easy to copy. What matters is building ecosystems around creators and developers that are hard to replicate, and investing in hard-to-copy assets like hardware.

> ⚡ **Maxim:** *"So much of consumer technology focuses on 'do I have product market fit?' People don't spend nearly enough time thinking about distribution."*

---

### 🏗️ Model 2: The Flat/Structured Dual-Organization

> 🧠 **The Concept:** Large organizations need hierarchy and operational rigor to deliver at scale, but that same hierarchy makes people risk-averse and focused on promotions rather than innovation. The most successful companies have both a flat, innovative team AND a structured, operational team—and the leader's job is to create healthy dialogue between them.

> 💡 **The Truth:** At Snap, there's a 9-12 person design team that operates completely flat, iterating rapidly and creating new things constantly. Meanwhile, the broader company serves nearly a billion customers and needs operational rigor. The magic happens in the dialogue between these two teams.

> ⚡ **Maxim:** *"If you want to have a good idea, you have to have lots of ideas."*

---

### 🛑 Model 3: Software Is Not a Moat

> 🧠 **The Concept:** 15 years ago, Snap learned that software features are easily cloned. Today, everyone is re-learning this with AI. The solution: build ecosystems (creator relationships, developer platforms) that are hard to copy, and invest in hardware that has high barriers to entry.

> 💡 **The Truth:** When features can be copied overnight, you need something more durable. Snap built a platform around AR where developers created millions of lenses—a full ecosystem that's much harder to replicate than a feature.

> ⚡ **Maxim:** *"Software is easy to copy. It's really important to build more durable modes."*

---

### 🫀 Model 4: Design as Intentional Bottleneck

> 🧠 **The Concept:** Design should operate as a bottleneck in the company—everything needs design approval before shipping. This annoys people and slows things down, but it results in a cohesive customer experience. When different teams own different parts of an app without a cohesive through-line, users can tell.

> 💡 **The Truth:** The alternative—shipping fast without design cohesion—creates apps that feel disjointed. Snap intentionally uses design as the bottleneck because they want to differentiate on product experience, not just ship features quickly.

> ⚡ **Maxim:** *"That bottleneck is really, really important because that's what results in a cohesive customer experience."*

---

### 🧭 Model 5: Humanity Over Technology

> 🧠 **The Concept:** The industry focuses on technology, but humanity is far more important because humanity dictates how technology gets adopted. People won't blindly adopt new technology—there's going to be massive societal pushback on many AI changes coming.

> 💡 **The Truth:** Tech leaders assume people will just adopt new technology as it comes out, but that's not how adoption works. The companies that win will be those that put humanity first in how they build and deploy technology.

> ⚡ **Maxim:** *"Humanity dictates how technology is adopted."*

# 🛠️ What They're Building / Focused On

---

## 🤖 AI Integration

> 🚀 **AI Co-pilot for Leadership:** Built an agent in Glean that combs through all company data—dashboards, documents, metrics—to surface what needs attention. Uses Claude to build agents that can take a product idea, write the spec, identify required sign-offs, do legal/trust-safety risk analysis, and create go-to-market materials in one shot.

> 🏃 **Designers Shipping Code:** Many designers at Snap now ship code directly. They've removed friction between ideation and impact at nearly-billion-user scale. Built AI guardrails and automated code review that has caught ~10,000 bugs.

> 🧪 **Jobs-to-Be-Done AI Organization:** Instead of letting a thousand AI projects bloom randomly, Snap organizes all AI work around "jobs to be done" for their community and advertisers—making it clear where agents can help and tracking business outcomes.

---

## 👓 Hardware (Spectacles)

> 🥽 **Specs 2026:** New AR glasses launching this year after 12 years of investment. Unlike heads-up display glasses that put a screen stuck to your face, Spectacles anchor content in the world—keeping users grounded and able to interact with friends, not just consuming content.

> 🎯 **The Vision:** Build technology that brings people together and keeps them grounded in the real world, rather than isolating them like phones do. People spend 7-8 hours a day on screens—something has to change.

> 🏗️ **Fully Vertically Integrated:** Built a fully vertically integrated stack around AR that's very hard for competitors to copy—combining hardware, operating system, and developer platform.

---

## 📱 Snapchat Product

> 📈 **Snapchat+:** 25M subscribers, over $1B revenue run rate—Meta noticed and copied with Instagram Plus.

> 🎮 **Gaming:** 200M people play games on Snapchat monthly—gaming is becoming a major engagement driver.

> 🔒 **Screenshot Detection:** Early innovation that helped Snapchat spread as a new way of communicating—detecting when recipients took screenshots before Apple provided any API for it.

---

> 👁️ **Middle child positioning:** Snap occupies an awkward middle position—bigger than Pinterest/Reddit but way smaller than Meta/Google. Defining themselves against both older and younger siblings has been historically challenging for "middle children."
