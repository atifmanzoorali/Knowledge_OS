# Introduction to Max Schoening

**Guest:** Max Schoening | **Role:** Head of Product at Notion | **Location:** San Francisco

---

## Executive Summary

Max Schoening is one of the most influential AI-forward product leaders in Silicon Valley. Previously a Product Manager at Google, Design Lead at Heroku, and Design Leader/Engineer at GitHub, Max now leads product at Notion where he has been instrumental in enabling designers and PMs to ship code. In this conversation with Lenny Muoio, Max shares his deep insights on agency in the AI era, the future of product building, and how Notion's AI agents became so successful.

Key themes: agency as the differentiating factor in AI era, the democratization of software building, taste as the last moat, and "malleable software" as the future of productivity tools.

---

# Key Insights

## 1. Agency is the New Superpower

Max believes agency—understanding that the world around you is malleable—is what separates those who thrive in the AI era from those who fall behind. He cites his own journey from being a designer who couldn't code to someone who now enables entire organizations to build with AI.

> "The world is made up by people no smarter than you. You can just change things."

He sees the biggest mistake people make as waiting for permission or assuming they lack some required skill. With AI removing technical barriers, the real differentiator is now **agency**, not technical ability.

---

## 2. The First 10% of Every Project is Now Free

One of Max's key frameworks: "The first 10% of every project is now free."

This fundamentally changes product development:
- No need to write lengthy PRDs when you can just build a janky prototype
- Demos > Memos
- "Shots on goal" increases because experimentation is cheap

> "It takes almost no effort to now build the first version of a startup."

---

## 3. Taste is the Last Moat

As AI handles more execution, **taste** becomes the competitive advantage:

> "Taste means you're able to run a virtual machine in your head where given an idea, you can predict whether a certain group will like it or not."

Building taste requires:
- Iterations with feedback
- Doing reps (like training a model)
- Side projects with full ownership
- Constant tinkering with new tools

---

## 4. Malleable Software

Max has been a pioneer of "malleable software"—software that works closer to the interest of users than the corporation building it.

> "Software works closer to the interest of the people that use it than the interest of the corporation that makes it."

Notion's success with AI stems from being a connected workspace where agents can "roam around" and access context—exactly what AI agents need. This was intentional, not accidental.

---

## 5. Why Notion AI Works

Max explains why Notion's AI agent succeeded:

1. **Context:** Notion has all company information in one place—perfect for AI to use
2. **Malleable foundation:** Built on blocks and slash commands, enabling AI versatility
3. **Timing:** First assistant launched before ChatGPT—AI-native from the start
4. **Enterprise search:** Automatic permission handling others don't do well

> "It was made for such a time as this."

---

## 6. The Jobs-to-be-Done Framework

Max uses Jobs-to-be-Done to zoom out and ask:

- What is the user hiring this product for?
- Would I even buy this if I were the user?
- Are we honest about what users actually want?

He admits he doesn't strongly identify with Christensen's original work but uses it as a reminder to **be the user** rather than an employee defending their creation.

---

## 7. The SaaS Apocalypse Debate

Max's hot take: The "SaaS apocalypse" (building everything yourself) is greatly exaggerated.

> "We already have universal basic income. It's called knowledge work."

His reasoning:
- People don't want to maintain the full stack
- Businesses want choice, not lock-in
- The "as a service" part is maintenance + specialists thinking hard about problems
- Like going to Costco—you want the steak, not to hunt it

---

## 8. What Makes Great Products

Max's framework for product success:

> "All the great products have something tiny that is a superpower. One tiny core that is so exceptionally good."

Examples:
- GitHub: Pull requests
- Heroku: `git push heroku master`
- Dropbox: Menu bar icon that syncs
- iPhone: Multi-touch

The pitfall: "If I just add one more thing, it will finally be great." **Never works.**

# What Max Would Do With AGI

> "I would be doing the exact same thing."

Max explains he's not code out of utility but intellectual challenge—like playing chess or go. Even if machines beat humans at coding, he'd keep doing it because it's inherently human to tinker and build.

---

# Contrarian Views

1. **Inclusivity isn't always great:** He's a believer in "small group theory"—the world runs by groups of 8 or fewer. Sometimes exclusivity beats trying to serve everyone.

2. **First doesn't matter:** Bluetooth headphones existed before AirPods. AirPods "just did it right." Being first is overrated.

3. **Token spend isn't a metric:** Bragging about tokens spent is like bragging about lines of code written. Not useful.

# Final Advice

Max's advice for young people in Silicon Valley:

1. Don't let the rush/fOMO distract from what you actually care about
2. Work incredibly hard from 18-25
3. Tune down the amplitude of worry—history repeats more than it changes completely
4. Just exercise agency—walk around your city or forest and realize everything around you was made by people no smarter than you

Within 6-9 months, you can figure out how to make most things around you from scratch.
