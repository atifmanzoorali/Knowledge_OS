# 📝 Full Context

This wisdom profile addresses one of humanity's most profound questions: Why does God allow good people to suffer? Drawing from Jewish tradition and Biblical narrative, this teaching explores the foundational story of Cain and Abel, Moses' confrontation with God, and the theological framework that explains suffering within the context of divine purpose.

The teaching comes from the Jewish Learning Institute and synthesizes insights from Rabbi Yanki Tauber, the Lubavitcher Rebbe, and other Jewish scholars. At its core, the message is that God deliberately chose not to intervene in human affairs to preserve our free will—the very capacity that makes love and goodness meaningful.

---

# 💎 Core Wisdom

## The Central Insight

> 🧠 **God allows suffering because love is a choice, and choice requires the possibility of choosing evil.**

The foundational answer from Jewish tradition is startling in its clarity: God could have created a world without suffering, but such a world would be devoid of genuine love, authentic goodness, and meaningful choice. The ability to choose evil is precisely what makes choosing good valuable. Without free will, love would be meaningless—forced compliance, not love.

---

## Key Principles

### 🎯 Free Will Is Non-Negotiable

> "God created us to grapple with the evil inclination and to choose good, and that entails the possibility of us choosing evil. We are given free will to make bad decisions, and it is exactly that option that makes good decisions good. If God were to prevent all evil—thereby stymieing free will—good would lose all meaning."

The story of Cain and Abel (Genesis 4) demonstrates this principle. God knew Cain harbored evil intent, yet He deliberately refrained from stopping the murder. Why? Because preventing the crime would have destroyed the very essence of human meaning—the struggle between good and evil inclinations that defines our humanity.

---

### 🎯 Protest Is an Act of Faith

> "Indeed, the question/protest/outcry, 'Why have You done evil to Your people?!' can issue only from the mouth of a true believer. The non-believer, too, may be outraged by the cruelty and suffering our world abounds with, but just who is he outraged at?"

Moses' protest to God (Exodus 5:22-23) wasn't a failure of faith—it was the highest form of faith. The Lubavitcher Rebbe taught that questioning God's ways in the face of injustice is precisely what a true believer does. It demonstrates belief in God's ultimate goodness and justice, even when faced with apparent contradiction.

---

### 🎯 Suffering Exists So We Can Redeem It

> "God has created an unredeemed world, and it is for us to redeem. He has put within us an imperfect character and it is for us to perfect. God will not do it for us. That would fly in the face of the entire Divine plan. It is man's job."

When bad things happen to good people, the question isn't "where was God?" The question is "where was man?" God was there, providing the opportunity to choose good. Our role is to redeem the world through our choices.

# 🛠️ Practical Application

## Daily Practices

- ✅ **Confront God Honestly:** Like Moses, bring your questions and protests directly to God. Don't suppress doubt—let it become a vehicle for deeper faith.

- ✅ **Choose Good Amid Struggle:** Recognize that the struggle between your good and evil inclinations is the very purpose of your existence. Embrace the battle as meaningful.

- ✅ **Ask "Where Was I?":** When faced with others' suffering, ask how you might be the answer to that suffering rather than questioning why God allowed it.

## Reflection Questions

> 🤔 When I face hardship, do I protest like Moses (with faith that good will prevail) or do I deny God's existence altogether?

> 🤔 Am I using my struggles as opportunities to choose good, or am I being overcome by the evil inclination?

> 🤔 How can I be part of redeeming the world rather than questioning why it needs redemption?
