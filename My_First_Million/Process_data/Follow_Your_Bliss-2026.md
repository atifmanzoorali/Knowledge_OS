# 📊 Executive Summary

This brainstorm episode tackles one of the most common questions from young people entering the workforce: "How do I find my passion?" Shaan argues that "follow your passion" is actually terrible advice and unpacks why the alternative—following your "bliss" or "blisters"—is far more actionable. The conversation explores Joseph Campbell's philosophy, Paul Graham's essays on enthusiasm as both engine and rudder, and introduces the powerful concept of finding your "loop"—the repeatable task that defines your work. The episode also covers the Top 5 Regrets of the Dying, why 70% of people hate their jobs, and practical frameworks for discovering what you should actually do with your life.

---

# 💡 Key Insights

> 💡 **"Follow your bliss, not your passion"** — Joseph Campbell originally said "follow your bliss" but later wished he'd said "follow your blisters." The key insight: look for evidence of willing suffering. If you're doing something that hurts but you keep doing it anyway, that's your signal.

> 💡 **The loop framework** — Don't pick an industry you love. Pick a "loop" you love. Every job can be broken down into a repeatable loop (founder loop, healer loop, farmer loop). Find the one that gives you energy, not the one that sounds impressive to others.

> 💡 **Enthusiasm as rudder, not just motor** — Paul Graham's framework: let enthusiasm be both the engine AND the rudder. It doesn't just fuel you—it guides you to the frontier of a field where you'll notice gaps that become opportunities.

> 💡 **Mastery creates passion** — Cal Newport's insight: passion is a byproduct of mastery, and mastery comes from enduring enthusiasm. You don't start passionate—you become passionate through sustained practice.

> 💡 **Pick a sales motion, not an industry** — Most time in business is spent on growth/sales, not product. Instead of "I love healthcare," ask "Do I like content marketing? Enterprise sales? SEO?" Pick the growth mechanism you enjoy.

> 💡 **The 70% problem** — According to Bill Gurley, 70% of people don't like how they spend their days. Half your waking life is work—shouldn't you find one you actually enjoy?

# 🏢 Business Ideas / Opportunities

> 💡 **Frontier opportunities** — When you follow enthusiasm to the frontier of any field, you notice gaps. Shaan's example: he got obsessed with fitness, reached the frontier, noticed the testosterone replacement gap, and that became a investment in Hone Health (now 9 figures).

> 💡 **Content-first businesses** — If you love content, pick businesses where content is the main growth channel. The work you do anyway becomes your competitive advantage.

> 💡 **Internal scorecard** — Build your own metrics so you're not comparing to external benchmarks (like $18M penthouses) that kill passion. The "pole to hold onto" metaphor: generic advice exists because the world will whip you around without it.

# 🚀 Lessons & Frameworks

> 🧠 **The Bliss vs. Blisters Framework:**
> - Bliss = what you're enthusiastic about doing
> - Blisters = the evidence of pain you're willing to endure
> - The key: look for willing suffering, not just pleasure

> 🧠 **The Loop Framework:**
> - Break any job into its repeatable loop
> - Example: founder loop = see status quo → imagine better → build product → sell → build team → repeat
> - Ask: "Do I love doing this loop thousands of times?"

> 🧠 **Enthusiasm as Frontier Finder:**
> - Enthusiasm takes you to the edge of a field
> - At the frontier, you notice gaps
> - Gaps = opportunities

> 🧠 **Name the Blisters:**
> - Before starting anything, list the suffering you'll endure
> - The blisters, not the rewards, determine if you'll stick with it

> 🧠 **The Art of Noticing:**
> - Others often see your enthusiasm before you do
> - Naval's mom noticed he was a businessman before he did
> - Adam Weisman's wife noticed he should be in real estate

# 💼 Startup Advice

> 🏃 **Don't pick a product you love—pick a growth motion you love.** The time spent on actual product work is minimal (maybe 15%). Most time is growth/sales/team-building. Love that loop or you'll hate your startup.

> 🏃 **Test the loop before committing.** You don't need to know upfront you'll love doing it 10,000 times. Try it once or twice. Ask: "Do I get energy from this or does it drain me?"

> 🏃 **Your passion doesn't have to be your job.** There's nothing wrong with having your passion be a hobby. The goal is to find a job you don't hate—one that gives you energy.

> 🏃 **Save 6-12 months of expenses before making big changes.** Financial security gives you options. Lack of money = unhappiness, regardless of passion.

# ⚡ Contrarian Views

> 🚩 **"Follow your passion" is terrible advice.** Most people don't know their passion (including Shaan at 36). The advice sounds good but is almost always useless. Better: follow your enthusiasm and look for evidence of willing suffering.

> 🚩 **Your job doesn't have to be your passion— it just has to not drain you.** The goal isn't to love every second of work. It's to find a loop that gives you energy rather than depletes it. 70% of people hate their jobs—that's the problem to solve.

> 🚩 **Pick "sales motion" over "industry."** Most founders pick industries they think will be fun, but 85% of their time goes to growth. The industry barely matters compared to the growth mechanism.
